Integration of technology into rehabilitation practice

Exploring clinicians’ perspectives of what helps or hinders uptake of new devices

Challenge
While rehabilitation technologies are being rapidly developed, many never find their way into routine clinical practice. UX is increasingly being used to bring the patient’s opinions into the design process, yet in the healthcare setting the clinician are often overlooked as users, despite their crucial role in the uptake of new devices. 
The clinician’s perspective has little exploration in the research. This project aimed to generate a research-based picture of what influences the integration of new rehabilitation technologies from the clinician’s perspective.
Approach
I used qualitative methodology for this research. I was responsible for ensuring ethical and cultural considerations were met, recruitment, interviewing participants and management of data. 
I had joint ownership of the content analysis and preliminary write up of results. 
I independently sought out opportunities to share the results with audiences from the design and UX community, as well as those in clinical settings.
Impact
Approaching the query from a person-centred philosophy helped us to add to the body of knowledge with a unique perspective. The findings went beyond identifying barriers and facilitators to uptake, and interrogated the value clinicians place on technology in rehabilitation. 
By presenting at UX events, I was able to build invaluable network connections.  I was approached by two large players in the health technology field in New Zealand to share these results with their teams. 
By considering the complexities for clinicians as a user group, the design and development of rehabilitation technologies can showcase their true value in clinical practice.
